# Task Type — Debate

The writer will produce a **steelmanned for/against treatment**
followed by a synthesis that commits to a view if evidence supports
one.  Your evidence must arm BOTH sides equally.

## What to gather

1. **For the proposition** — gather **3+ strong arguments and pieces
   of evidence** supporting the claim.  Look for the strongest case,
   not strawmen.
2. **Against the proposition** — gather **3+ strong arguments and
   pieces of evidence** opposing the claim.  Same standard — best
   arguments, not weakest.
3. **Common ground** — facts both sides agree on.  Useful for the
   writer's synthesis section.
4. **Meta-evidence** — surveys of expert opinion, polls, prevalence
   data showing how the debate is currently divided.

## Source priorities

- **Identifiable advocates** for each side, not anonymous opinion.
- **Original sources** — if a quote is famous, find the speaker /
  paper / publication where it first appeared.
- **Both academic and popular sources** — debates often play out
  differently in scholarly literature vs public discourse.

## Cross-checking

For high-stakes debates, **verify quotes and statistics directly**.
Misattributed quotes and out-of-context stats poison synthesis.  If
a stat appears in multiple secondary sources, trace it to the primary.

## Evidence chunks

- Tag chunks by side via ``source_type`` or in the ``snippet``
  (e.g. start with "PRO:" or "CON:") so the writer can route them
  correctly.
- Include the speaker / author when known — the writer will cite by
  attribution ("X argues that ...[3]").

## Common pitfalls

- Asymmetric coverage (5 for, 1 against).  The writer can't
  synthesise from imbalanced evidence — neither can you assess
  whether the debate is genuinely contested or settled.
- Strawmanning the side you personally disagree with.  Gather the
  best version of the opposing case.
- Treating opinion as fact.  Debates often hinge on values, not
  evidence — surface this distinction.
